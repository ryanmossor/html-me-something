Introduction

Welcome to my web page! This page contains a brief overview about my history with speedrunning, a hobby I’ve been involved with since 2013. I’ll first cover how I became interested in speedrunning before delving into some of the specific aspects of speedrunning I enjoy the most.

Before we begin though, what even is speedrunning? Speedrunning is a style of playing a video game wherein the purpose is to complete a set of objectives as quickly as possible (e.g., beat the game as quickly as possible or collect all in-game items as quickly as possible). Some speedrunners are extremely dedicated to their pursuit to become the fastest at their respective game; it’s not uncommon for top contenders in a game to have several thousand attempts under their belts.

Sometimes, people are confused when I tell them this is one of my hobbies. I’ve been asked, “Why would you ever want to play the same game over and over again? Don’t you get bored?” I certainly understand this perspective, but in my experience, speedrunning has added limitless depth and replay value to my favorite games. Just as it’s enjoyable to watch yourself improve at basketball, chess, or programming, it’s also fun to keep getting faster and more skilled at a video game you love to play.

Über Challenge

In 2008, before I even knew what speedrunning was, I had just finished replaying through The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time with my friend Jacob. After finishing the game, Jacob was looking on the GameFAQs website for additional information and secrets. He found a document for something called the "Ocarina of Time Uber Challenge" and suggested we try it out.

The Uber Challenge was a self-imposed challenge that required players to complete the game with no health upgrades, no shield, a broken sword, and no deaths, among a number of other much more difficult restrictions. To meet the challenge’s requirements, you needed to exploit game mechanics and complete many things out of their intended sequence. It took me nearly two months to complete the challenge, and some of the required tricks and exploits took me several hours of attempts to pull off successfully.

Though the Uber Challenge was frustrating at times, I was also interested by what was possible in Ocarina of Time with glitches and exploits. I played through the game probably 10 more times during 2008, each time learning new techniques and doing different parts of the game out of order. I also discovered speedruns while searching for new techniques, since speedrunners often use glitches and exploits to play through the game faster. I was fascinated watching people quickly execute difficult techniques and finish the game faster than I thought possible.

AGDQ 2013 and Renewed Interest

After 2008, Jacob and I moved on to other interests, and I didn’t keep up with speedrunning much between then and 2013. However, in March 2013 I came across a YouTube video of an Ocarina of Time speedrun performed by a runner named Cosmo. This run was done at Awesome Games Done Quick 2013, a yearly speedrunning marathon event which showcases a full week’s worth of speedruns to raise money for charity.

I remember being awestruck by several aspects of Cosmo’s run. In 2008, speedruns of Ocarina of Time were typically segmented (i.e., they were done in short chunks and strung together in a YouTube playlist, which allowed runners to keep retrying for optimal segments), and a full run took several hours from start to finish. By contrast, Cosmo’s run was single-segment, contained excellent commentary about the run and the game’s speedrun history, was performed in front of a live audience, and was completed in just 22 minutes.

I was blown away by Cosmo’s run and how far Ocarina of Time speedruns had come in the past 5 years, and I soon found myself spending most of my free time watching videos and livestreams of speedruns of Ocarina of Time and other games.

Bingo and Racing

After watching speedruns regularly for a few months, I gradually started playing around with techniques in Ocarina of Time for the first time in years. I revisited some of the old tricks I knew from 2008, but also tried replicating some of the newer techniques I’d seen in more recent runs. At the time, I never intended to start speedrunning myself, but it wasn’t long before I joined my first race against other Ocarina of Time community members.

In August 2013, I joined my first Ocarina of Time bingo race and quickly became hooked. In a bingo race, all players are given the same randomly-generated board of in-game objectives (e.g., collect a certain item, beat a specific dungeon, etc.), and the goal is to get a “bingo” by completing all five objectives in a given row, column, or diagonal on the board.

I loved the unique nature of bingo races. No two bingo boards are the same, and becoming proficient enough at the game to feel comfortable doing any given row requires a ton of game knowledge, execution skill, and ability to plan things out on-the-fly.

I had to quit my first few bingo races since I didn’t know enough to finish in a reasonable amount of time, and my first few finishes took roughly three hours (a good bingo time is around 1:15:00). I kept at it, though, and slowly became better as I practiced more and learned faster techniques. I still remember how rewarding it was to finish my first bingo races in under two hours, under 1:30:00, and under 1:20:00.

Ocarina of Time Championship Series

Aside from bingo races, some of my favorite Ocarina of Time experiences are from the Ocarina of Time Championship Series (OCS). OCS is a series of weekly challenge races that require players to complete a specific objective, often while imposing a variety of restrictions. Like bingo, challenge races also test players' overall game knowledge, execution ability, and planning capabilities.

Players are awarded points based on how well they perform in each race, and the top 16 overall players at the end of the season compete in the OCS Playoffs to determine the winner. Unfortunately, I missed all of Season 1 because of school, and I finished in 17th place overall in Season 2. I’ll get to try my hand at the playoffs this season, though, since I finished in the top 16 out of 70+ total entrants throughout the season. The standard races for Season 3 just finished on February 28, and playoff races start on March 14.

Conclusion

In summary, I’m grateful for all that speedrunning has provided me over the past seven years. Beyond just being a fun pastime, it’s introduced me to some of my closest friends, with whom I’ve spent hundreds of hours racing and bantering. I’ve even had the opportunity to travel to AGDQ events a few times myself, where I’ve met these friends in person. Each time I’ve gone to an AGDQ, it’s been one of the greatest experiences of my life.

These days, I don’t speedrun nearly as much as I used to. Most of my friends I made through racing have gotten busy with life and have moved on, and I also haven’t been able to spend as much time with it due to other obligations. However, I have a good friend I still do bingo races with every now and then, and I’ve fortunately been able to play through the entirety of the OCS season this year.
