Introduction

Welcome to my website! This page contains a brief overview about my history with speedrunning, a hobby I’ve been involved with since 2013.

Before we begin, though, what even is speedrunning? Speedrunning is a style of playing video games wherein the purpose is to complete the game as quickly as possible.

Sometimes, people are confused when I tell them this is one of my hobbies. I’ve been asked, “Don't you get bored of playing the same game over and over?” While I understand this perspective, speedrunning has added limitless depth and replay value to my favorite games. Just as it’s enjoyable to watch yourself improve at basketball, chess, or programming, it’s also fun to keep getting faster and more skilled at a video game you love to play.

Ocarina of Time and the "Uber Challenge"

In 2008, before I even knew what speedrunning was, I had just finished replaying through The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time with my friend Jacob. While searching the Internet for trivia and secrets about the game, Jacob found something called the "Ocarina of Time Uber Challenge" and suggested we try it out.

The Uber Challenge was a self-imposed challenge that required players to complete the game with no health upgrades, no shield, a broken sword, no deaths, and several other much more difficult restrictions. To do this, you needed to exploit game mechanics and complete many things out of their intended sequence. The challenge took me nearly two months, and some of the tricks and exploits took me many hours of attempts to pull off successfully.

Though the Uber Challenge was frustrating at times, I was also fascinated by what was possible in Ocarina of Time with glitches and exploits. I played through the game 10-15 more times during 2008, learning new tricks upon each subsequent playthrough. I also discovered speedruns while learning new techniques, since speedrunners often use glitches and exploits to play through the game faster.

AGDQ 2013 and Renewed Interest

After 2008, Jacob and I moved on to other interests, and I didn't really follow speedrunning for a few years. However, in March 2013 I came across a YouTube video of an Ocarina of Time speedrun performed by a runner named Cosmo. This run was done at Awesome Games Done Quick 2013, a yearly speedrunning marathon event which showcases a full week’s worth of speedruns to raise money for charity.

In 2008, speedruns of Ocarina of Time were typically segmented (i.e., they were done in short chunks and strung together in a YouTube playlist, which allowed runners to keep retrying for optimal segments), and a full run took several hours from start to finish.

By contrast, Cosmo’s run was done in one sitting, was performed in front of a live audience, contained excellent commentary about the run and the game’s speedrun history, and was completed in just 22 minutes.

I was amazed by Cosmo’s run and how far Ocarina of Time speedruns had come in the past five years, and I soon found myself frequently watching videos and livestreams of speedruns of Ocarina of Time and other games.

Bingo and Racing

After watching speedruns for a few months, I started playing around in Ocarina of Time for the first time in years. I revisited some of the old tricks I knew from 2008, but also tried out some of the newer techniques I’d seen in more recent runs. I never intended to start speedrunning myself, but it wasn’t long before I joined my first race against other Ocarina of Time community members.

In August 2013, I joined my first Ocarina of Time bingo race and quickly became hooked. In a bingo race, all players are given the same randomly-generated board of in-game objectives, and the goal is to get a “bingo” by completing all five objectives in a given row, column, or diagonal on the board.

I loved the unique nature of bingo races. No two bingo boards are the same, and becoming proficient enough at the game to feel comfortable doing any given row requires a ton of game knowledge, execution skill, and ability to plan things out on-the-fly.

I quit my first few races since I didn’t know enough to finish in a reasonable amount of time, and my first few finishes took nearly three hours (a good bingo time is around 1:15:00). I kept at it, though, and slowly became better as I practiced more and learned faster techniques. It was incredibly rewarding to see myself steadily improve and surpass the sub-two-hour, sub-1:30:00, and sub-1:20:00 milestones.

Ocarina of Time Championship Series

Aside from bingo races, some of my favorite Ocarina of Time experiences are from the Ocarina of Time Championship Series (OCS). OCS is a series of weekly challenge races that require players to complete a specific objective, often while imposing a variety of restrictions. Like bingo, challenge races also test players' overall game knowledge, execution ability, and planning capabilities.

Players are awarded points based on how well they perform in each race, and the top 16 overall players at the end of the season compete in the OCS Playoffs to determine the winner. After missing the Season 2 playoffs by a single placement, I'll get to compete in the playoffs for this season since I finished in the top 10 out of 70+ total entrants throughout the season. The standard races for Season 3 just finished on February 28, and playoff races start on March 14!

Conclusion

In summary, I’m grateful for all that speedrunning has provided me over the past seven years. Beyond just being a fun pastime, it’s introduced me to some of my closest friends, with whom I’ve spent hundreds of hours racing and bantering. I’ve even had the opportunity to travel to AGDQ events a few times myself, where I’ve met these friends in person. Each time I’ve gone to an AGDQ, it’s been one of the greatest experiences of my life.

These days, I don’t speedrun nearly as much as I used to. Most of my friends I made through racing have gotten busy with life and have moved on, and I also haven’t been able to spend as much time with it due to other obligations. However, I still do bingo races with a good friend every now and then, and I’ve fortunately been able to play through the entirety of the OCS season this year.
